Challenges Facing Children

Many children drop out due to poverty, child labor, and limited access to quality education, especially in rural and conflict-affected areas. Girls are particularly at risk, facing gender inequality and early marriage that disrupt their education.

Global Distribution of Out-of-School Rates

Children are deprived of education for a range of reasons, but poverty remains one of the most persistent and difficult barriers to overcome.
Gender inequality is another critical factor — globally, an estimated 129 million girls are out of school, with many facing obstacles such as early marriage, gender-based violence, and limited access to menstrual hygiene facilities. In some regions, girls are 1.5 times more likely than boys to be excluded from secondary education. Children living amid economic hardship, political unrest, conflict, or natural disasters are also at greater risk of being cut off from schooling, as are those with disabilities or from marginalized ethnic groups.

Correlation Between Out-of-School Rates and Life Expectancy

There is a clear negative correlation between out-of-school rates and life expectancy at birth — countries with higher life expectancy tend to have lower rates of children out of school.

This suggests a meaningful link between education access and health outcomes. However, it's important to note that not all countries with high out-of-school rates necessarily have a high proportion of people living in rural areas.

Economic Factors and Education

Higher out-of-school rates are often linked to:

Lower GDP per capita
Higher inflation rates

Higher out-of-school rates are often linked to lower GDP per capita and higher inflation, showing how poverty and instability limit access to education. Rising inflation can lead to budget cuts, lower household income, and increased dropout rates. In contrast, economically stable countries with low inflation tend to offer more reliable access to education.

Conclusion

Between 2000 and 2020, the number of out-of-school children of upper secondary age fell from 175 million to 132 million, showing important progress, though millions of children, especially in vulnerable areas, still remain excluded. There is a strong negative correlation between out-of-school rates and life expectancy, highlighting education's critical role in improving health outcomes and societal development. Poverty, gender inequality, conflict, and economic hardship continue to be major barriers, with girls and children in rural or crisis-affected regions at greatest risk. Addressing structural inequalities and ensuring economic stability are essential for creating inclusive, resilient education systems that enable all children to complete their schooling.

Solutions

Solutions involve increasing targeted financial aid, implementing programs that specifically support girls' continued education, building resilient education services in conflict-affected areas, and stabilizing national economies to safeguard schooling opportunities. Strengthening financial support mechanisms can ease the burden on low-income families and reduce dropout rates. Prioritizing gender-sensitive policies ensures that girls are not left behind due to societal or cultural barriers. Focusing on economic resilience and protecting education funding during crises is essential to guarantee that all children can access and complete their schooling.
